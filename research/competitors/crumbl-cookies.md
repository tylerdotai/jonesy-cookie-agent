# Competitor: Crumbl Cookies

**Type:** National franchise chain (rapidly scaling)
**Founded:** 2017, Logan UT
**Locations:** 1,100+ in US and Canada
**Revenue:** ~$1B (2024)
**Model:** Rotating weekly menu, open kitchen, pink box, delivery + pickup

---

## What They Do Well

### 1. Weekly Rotating Menu (The FOMO Engine)
This is Crumbl's core innovation. They rotate 6 classic cookies + 4 new flavors every week. Customers check the menu every Thursday (or wherever their cycle lands) and decide if they want to go. This creates:
- Repeat visits (you HAVE to go THIS week)
- Social sharing (new flavor = content)
- Discovery (try something you've never had before)
- Conversation ("what'd you get at Crumbl?")

The rotation IS the marketing. It costs almost nothing to execute and generates continuous organic engagement.

### 2. Social Media as a Business Function
Crumbl doesn't just market on social — they built the brand ON social. TikTok videos of cookies pulling apart, frosting tips, behind-the-scenes of the open kitchen. Their content IS the product experience. 1.3M+ TikTok followers generating free impressions constantly.

### 3. Signature Pink Box
The pink box is iconic. It serves no functional purpose — it's purely brand theater. But the unboxing moment generates user content. People post their pink boxes. It's a $0 marketing asset that pays dividends.

### 4. Open Kitchen Theater
Crumbl shows you the baking. The open kitchen isn't just efficient — it's content. Watching cookies bake is genuinely satisfying content. They turned the production process into a marketing asset.

### 5. Bundled Pricing Psychology
Four-pack and six-pack pricing at a perceived discount creates a psychological anchor. $4/cookie is a lot. $12 for 4 feels like a deal. The bundle is almost always the default order. This drives AUV up while seeming like a value to the customer.

### 6. Vertical Integration
Crumbl HQ controls dough production and distribution centrally. Each franchisee gets pre-mixed, pre-portioned dough. This means:
- Consistent quality across locations
- No local baker skill required
- Scalable operations
- Franchise model that actually works at scale

### 7. "Apple Store of Cookies" Experience
Clean stores, polite staff, smooth checkout, bright lighting. The experience of buying a Crumbl cookie is intentionally frictionless and pleasant. They benchmarked themselves against Apple retail.

---

## Where They Could Improve

### 1. Cookie Quality Has Declined
Reddit and consumer sentiment increasingly say Crumbl quality has dropped — same flavor profiles recycled, ingredients feel cheap, cookies don't taste as good as they did in early years. The volume-at-all-costs model is starting to show in product quality.

### 2. Same 6 Flavors Forever
Despite weekly "rotations," the same base profiles repeat endlessly: chocolate chip, s'mores, strawberry cobbler, etc. The novelty wears off. Customers report frustration that every "new" flavor is just a rehash of existing ingredients in a different combination.

### 3. No Real Local Identity
Crumbl is corporate to its core. Every location looks the same, acts the same, tastes the same. There's no baker story, no local pride, no community connection. You're buying from a franchise machine, not a neighbor.

### 4. Delivery Is Expensive
Crumbl's delivery through their app or DoorDash adds significant cost to an already-priced product. The core "treat yourself" impulse buy is dampened by a $6-8 delivery fee on top of cookie prices.

### 5. No Custom Orders or Events
Crumbl doesn't do weddings, parties, or custom decorated cookies. Their model is "come to us or order delivery." They have no event presence whatsoever. Every wedding, birthday, and corporate event they lose is simply not their market.

### 6. Health-Conscious Gap
Crumbl cookies are enormous, rich, sugar-forward, and not trying to be anything else. There's no offer for "I want something smaller/less sweet/healthier-ish." They own one category (massive gourmet cookie) and ignore the adjacent ones.

## How Jonesy's Can Incorporate This

| Crumbl Tactic | Jonesy's Implementation |
|---------------|------------------------|
| Weekly flavor rotation | Agent-managed "Flavor of the Week" — new seasonal drop every 1-2 weeks, announced on social, limited time |
| Pink box moment | Create a signature Jonesy's branded box or packaging that shows up in event photos. Something worth posting |
| Open kitchen theater | Post behind-the-scenes baking videos on Instagram. Real cookies, real process, no studio production needed |
| Social content strategy | Agent generates 5-7 posts/week automatically — cookie photos, flavor reveals, event setups |
| Bundling psychology | Offer a "Cookie Platter" bundle — 3 dozen mixed flavors at a price that feels like a deal vs. buying individually |
| Subscription model | "Jonesy's Monthly Cookie Club" — recurring order for offices or individuals. Auto-renews, always has fresh cookies |
| Event presence | Show up to every ClawPlex meetup, every local event, every farmers market. Consistent presence builds trust |

---

## Jonesy's Advantage Over Crumbl

Crumbl is a machine. Jonesy's is a person. Crumbl can produce 1,000 identical cookies. Jonesy's can produce one perfect custom cookie for a wedding. Crumbl is built for the grab-and-go impulse buy. Jonesy's is built for the event that matters.

**The play:** Don't try to beat Crumbl on the weekly rotation or the pink box. Own the "this was made for YOUR event" category. Let Crumbl have the late-night snack market. Own the celebration market.

---

## Threat Level for Jonesy's

**Medium.** Crumbl is a real threat in the DFW market — they have multiple locations and strong brand awareness among the exact age group (18-35) that attends ClawPlex meetups. They WILL come up when someone searches "cookies Fort Worth."

However:
- Crumbl doesn't do events, weddings, or custom orders
- Crumbl has no community presence
- Crumbl is increasingly seen as corporate and low-quality
- Crumbl's delivery model is expensive

Jonesy's should compete on **quality + relationship**, not on convenience or novelty. Let Crumbl own the "I want cookies right now" market. Own the "I want cookies that were made for my event" market.
